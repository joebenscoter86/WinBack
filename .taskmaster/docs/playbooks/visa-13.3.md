# Visa 13.3 — Not as Described / Defective Merchandise

> Draft playbook for Joe's review. Maps to PRD playbook schema.

## Metadata
- **Network:** Visa
- **Reason Code:** 13.3
- **Display Name:** Not as Described or Defective Merchandise/Services
- **Legacy Code:** 53

---

## Description (Plain English)

The cardholder claims the merchandise they received was defective, damaged, not as described, or that services didn't match what was promised. This is the most subjective dispute type — "not as described" is in the eye of the beholder. The good news: as of October 2024, **cardholders must attempt to return the merchandise before filing**, which gives merchants a stronger defense if no return was attempted.

---

## Issuer Evaluation (What the Bank Looks At)

1. **Does the product match its description?** The bank compares what was advertised/described against what was received.
2. **Did the cardholder attempt to return the merchandise?** (Required as of Oct 2024)
3. **If no return attempt, why not?** The cardholder must provide documented reasons why return was impossible.
4. **Did the merchant offer a resolution?** Replacement, repair, refund, store credit?
5. **Was the merchant responsive to complaints?** Unresponsive merchants lose by default.
6. **Filing window:** 120 calendar days from transaction date. Merchant has 30 days to respond.
7. **For subjective claims:** The issuer may require a **neutral third-party opinion** to corroborate the cardholder's claim.

The issuer leans toward the cardholder on quality disputes **unless** the merchant has firm evidence that the product matched its description. Your product listing and pre-shipment documentation are your primary defense.

## Evidence Checklist

### Mandatory Evidence
| Item | Required | Why It Matters |
|------|----------|----------------|
| Product description as shown at time of purchase (screenshot, listing page) | **Yes** | This is what the bank compares against the cardholder's claim. If the description matches what was sent, you win. |
| Proof of delivery (tracking, delivery confirmation) | **Yes** | Must first prove the item was delivered before you can argue it matched the description. |

### Strongly Recommended
| Item | Required | Why It Matters |
|------|----------|----------------|
| Pre-shipment photos of the actual item | Recommended | Proves the item was in good condition when shipped. Critical for "arrived damaged" claims. |
| Product specifications / detailed listing | Recommended | The more specific your listing, the harder it is for the cardholder to claim "not as described." |
| Customer communications about the product | Recommended | Shows any discussions about the product, complaints, or resolution attempts. |
| Proof cardholder did NOT attempt to return | Recommended | As of Oct 2024, the cardholder must attempt a return first. If they didn't, highlight this. |
| Return policy clearly stated at checkout | Recommended | Proves the cardholder knew how to return the item. |
| Refund/replacement confirmation (if already issued) | Helpful | Ends the dispute if you already resolved it. |

### For Services
| Item | Required | Why It Matters |
|------|----------|----------------|
| Service agreement / scope of work | **Yes** | Defines exactly what was promised. |
| Proof of service delivery (reports, access logs, deliverables) | **Yes** | Shows the service was performed as agreed. |
| Client sign-off or acceptance | Recommended | If the client approved the work, they can't claim it wasn't as described. |

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| Vague or exaggerated product descriptions | If your listing says "premium quality" and the product is average, you've set yourself up to lose. Be accurate and specific. |
| No pre-shipment documentation | Without photos or inspection records, you can't prove the item was fine when it left your warehouse. |
| Ignoring customer complaints | If the cardholder emailed you about a defective product and you didn't respond, the bank will side with them. Always respond, even if you disagree. |
| Refusing to accept returns | If your return policy is "no returns" but the item is defective, you lose. Visa's rules override your return policy for defective merchandise. |
| Not providing return instructions | The cardholder must attempt a return, but they need to know HOW. If you didn't provide return instructions, this requirement is weakened. |
| Poor packaging causing transit damage | If the item was fine when shipped but arrived damaged due to bad packaging, that's on you. Document your packaging process. |

## Pro Tips

- **Screenshot your product listings regularly.** Listings change over time, but the bank evaluates against what was shown when the customer purchased. Save timestamped copies.
- **Photograph items before shipping.** Quick phone photos showing item condition + packaging. Takes 30 seconds, saves you hundreds.
- **Respond to every complaint within 24 hours.** Even if you can't resolve it immediately, acknowledge the issue. "We're looking into this" documented in email is evidence of responsiveness.
- **Leverage the Oct 2024 return requirement.** If the cardholder filed without attempting a return, this is a strong defense point. Ask: "Did the cardholder attempt to return the item? Our return policy is clearly stated and we have no record of a return request."
- **For subjective claims ("quality not good enough"), neutral third-party opinions help.** If you have professional quality certifications, testing reports, or industry standards compliance, include them.
- **Offer a resolution before the dispute escalates.** A $20 partial refund is cheaper than a $150 chargeback + $15 fee.

## Urgency Essentials (When Deadline is <5 Days)

Focus on these in order:
1. Product listing / description at time of purchase
2. Delivery confirmation
3. Any customer communication (especially where no return was attempted)
4. Pre-shipment photos if available
